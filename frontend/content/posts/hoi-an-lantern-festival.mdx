## The Night the Power Goes Out on Purpose

On the 14th day of every lunar month, something unusual happens in Hội An: the town turns off its electric lights.

Street lamps go dark. Shop neon signs cut out. And in their place, thousands of silk lanterns ignite — red, gold, green, indigo — strung across every alley, balanced on bamboo poles above doorways, floated down the Thu Bồn River on tiny paper boats with tea-light flames at their centres.

The Hội An Lantern Festival has been running for over two decades, but it taps into something much older: a Buddhist lunar tradition of releasing light onto water to honour ancestors and welcome good fortune. For one night a month, the 15th-century trading port becomes completely itself again.

## Walking the Ancient Town After Dark

The UNESCO-listed Ancient Town covers roughly one square kilometre — small enough to walk end to end in twenty minutes, large enough to lose yourself in completely.

Start at **Hội An Market** on the east end and walk west along **Trần Phú Street**, the oldest road in town. At festival time this street becomes almost impassable: tour groups, solo travellers, local families, children clutching lanterns they've just bought from street vendors for around 20,000 VND (less than $1).

The buildings along Trần Phú are a living architectural record — Chinese merchant houses with their signature barrel-vault roofs sit beside Japanese-influenced covered walkways and French colonial shopfronts with terracotta tiles. In lantern light they look like a stage set, except nothing here is a replica.

At the western end, the **Japanese Covered Bridge** (Chùa Cầu) is the festival's unofficial centrepiece. Built in 1593 by the Japanese community to connect their quarter to the Chinese merchants', it's small — maybe fifteen metres long — but it draws the biggest crowds. ## The Thu Bồn River

If the Ancient Town is where you walk, the **Thu Bồn River** is where you sit and watch.

The riverfront promenade fills up from around 6pm. Vendors set up low plastic stools and sell *bia hơi* (draught beer, very cold, about 10,000 VND a glass), fresh sugarcane juice, and grilled corn. The atmosphere is unhurried — locals treat festival night less as a tourist spectacle and more as a collective exhale.

For 50,000–80,000 VND you can buy a **flower lantern boat** (thuyền hoa đăng) from one of the women who wade into the shallows at the river's edge. You make a wish, light the tea candle, and set it on the water. Hundreds of these flames drift slowly downstream together. It's one of those rare travel moments that photographs can't quite capture and that you understand, while it's happening, you'll be trying to explain to people for years.

## Food: What to Eat and Where

Hội An has one of the most distinctive local cuisines in Vietnam, and festival night is the best time to eat it — the streets are full of street food that's harder to find during the day.

**Cao Lầu** is the dish most associated with Hội An and the one you should not leave without trying. It's a noodle dish — thick rice noodles with char siu-style pork, bean sprouts, fresh herbs, and crispy fried wonton strips, served with just a little broth. The flavour is deep and savoury; the texture is everything at once. The noodles are traditionally made with water from a specific local well, which is either a beautiful piece of food mythology or genuinely explains why cao lầu tastes different here than anywhere else. **Bánh Mì Phượng** on Phan Châu Trinh Street has been called the best bánh mì in the world by several food writers, including Anthony Bourdain. Whether or not that's true, the queue at festival time tends to reach the street, which is its own kind of endorsement.

**White Rose Dumplings** (Bánh Vạc) are delicate, translucent steamed dumplings filled with shrimp and shallots, topped with crispy shallot oil. They're sold at a few specific restaurants and — like cao lầu — the recipe is said to belong to a single family. [ADD: current restaurant name and address]

## Final Thoughts

Hội An on lantern night is one of those experiences that operates on two levels simultaneously: it's genuinely beautiful as spectacle, and it's also — if you let it be — quietly meaningful. The act of releasing a lit boat onto dark water and watching it join hundreds of others drifting toward the sea is the kind of thing that stays with you.

The town will be busy. There will be other tourists. Someone nearby will be watching everything through a phone screen. None of that matters much, because the lanterns are real, the river is real, and the light on the water is real.

Go on a weeknight if you can. Arrive early. Eat the cao lầu. Float your boat.
